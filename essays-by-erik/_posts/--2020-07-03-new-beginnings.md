I’m about to start as Director of Engineering for [Strata Oncology](https://www.strataoncology.com/){:target="_blank"}. Ahead of my start I’ve been reflecting on what successes I may repeat and what failures I may avoid from my time at [Duo Security](https://duo.com/){:target="_blank"} after 5+ years.

It’s a different company, a different culture, a different (to be formed) team.

## Principles

Here are a few of the principles that are top of mind for me to apply going in. Some are innate in my behaviors, others require conscious effort on my part. I wrote them down so I could refer back to them if I need a waypoint.

* **Say “good morning”**: I’m leading remotely during the middle of a pandemic. Physical and communication cures are lacking. Show them I’m human and present.
* **Listen, don’t assume**: There will be a lot that I don’t know or understand, a lot of context I lack. Be patient.
* **Focus on high-leverage activities**: It will be easy to fall for the feeling of being busy and diving into work that feels good. In particular, shy away from deep technical work. It’s much more important to hire more software engineers than sling code.
* **Work on the business**: See above.
* **Manage towards the result you want; hope is not a strategy.**
* **Have a bias towards action; have a sense of urgency.**
* **For long feedback loops, focus on the inputs and not the results**: There will come a time when I’m not sure if I’m doing the right things. I remember recruiting my first engineer for Duo Austin was taking longer than I’d like. I wasn’t sure if I was doing the right things. Zack Urlocker, our COO, framed recruiting as an inputs-based system. If it takes 100 LinkedIn messages to get a response and 10 responses to get to a candidate that we want to bring onsite and 3 onsite to a hire and if we want to hire 3 people, we know the numbers we need to hit. Eventually we can adjust from the results, but some activities are inherently long cycle.
* **Take a beat; slow down your criticism**: A former manager once told me in our final 1:1 before he departed that the #1 thing I could do to improve is to slow down my criticism. It’s natural for engineers to be critical. But hear what’s being said first. Sleep on your disagreement. 90% of the time it will dissipate. And the other 10% of the time, you’ll figure out how to frame your solution to bring others along.
* **There is no _one_ perfect / right answer; perfect is the enemy of good enough.**
* **Even if you know the “right” answer, ask others**: It goes much better to front load the work of bringing others along in your ideas.

## Priorities

With that said, here’s my priority thesis for my first 12 months at Strata. They’re in rough order but not strictly linear.

* **Hire a team**: Not much else matters until we have the horsepower to get things done without being destroyed by interrupts.
* **Provide onboarding and career structure for them**: People never “hit the ground running”. It’s higher leverage to help the team learn new systems and the API of the org before we focus on net new work.
* **Build out a product roadmap**: Now that we’re cooking with gas, let’s take this thing for a drive. That's how metaphors work, right?
* **Tune functioning of engineering**: We should be doing the right work at this point, now we refine how we get work done. The capabilities of [Accelerate](https://www.amazon.com/Accelerate-Software-Performing-Technology-Organizations/dp/1942788339){:target="_blank"} come to mind. 

> Closing time. Time for you to go out to the places you will be from.
